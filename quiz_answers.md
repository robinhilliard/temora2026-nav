- **Inferred** - consistent with multiple sources but no source

## Item 10 - First landing at Cootamundra, 5 April 1919

Two DH-6 aircraft landed at Cootamundra en route Melbourne -> Sydney.

### 10a "XXXXXXX Paddocks" - what was the original name of the field?

**Answer: Quinlan's Paddock.** **Confidence: Confirmed.**

- The Australian Government purchased about 30 ha (75 acres) of
  **Quinlan's paddock** on the northern edge of Cootamundra in 1921,
  "making Cootamundra one of NSW's earliest rural aerodromes". [1]
- Hint cross-check: an airport road carries the name. **Quinlan Drive**
  runs along the south side of Cootamundra Airport (see for example the
  real-estate listing for 9 Quinlan Drive, Cootamundra NSW 2590, which
  describes a hangar opening onto a grassed strip that leads onto the
  sealed runway). [2]

### 10b One of the two pilots later flew record-breaking flights - who?

**Answer: _____________________** **Confidence: Needs user source.**

No web source I checked names the 5 April 1919 DH-6 Melbourne-Sydney
crews. The Wikipedia treatments of Cootamundra and the Cootamundra
WWII Fuel Depot start the aviation timeline with W. J. Stutt's 1917
Curtiss landing near the showground [1] and then jump to the Smith
brothers' Vimy arrival on 23 February 1920 [3]. The Encyclopedia of
Aircraft DH-6 entry [4] lists known Australian DH-6 movements but does
not mention a Melbourne-Sydney pair in April 1919. See
[Open questions](#open-questions-for-the-user) below for the local
sources most likely to settle this.

## Item 11 - 1930s rest-stop visitors (circle those that landed at Cootamundra)

| # | Pilot / operator                       | Aircraft                                | Landed at Cootamundra?    | Confidence  | Sources |
|---|----------------------------------------|-----------------------------------------|---------------------------|-------------|---------|
| a | P. G. Taylor                           | DH-6                                    | No                        | Inferred    | [4]     |
| b | Sir Ross & Sir Keith Smith             | Vickers Vimy *G-EAOU*                   | Yes (23 Feb 1920)         | Confirmed   | [3]     |
| c | Bert Hinkler                           | Avro Avian *G-EBOV*                     | Yes (Mar 1928, ~30 min)   | Confirmed   | [5]     |
| d | Sir Charles Kingsford Smith            | Fokker F.VIIb/3m *Southern Cross VH-USU*| Probably                  | Inferred    | [6][7]  |
| e | KLM                                    | Douglas DC-2 *Uiver PH-AJU*             | Yes (return leg, late 1934)| Confirmed  | [8]     |
| f | Freda Thompson                         | DH-87 Hornet Moth                       | No (wrong aircraft / era) | Confirmed   | [9]     |
| g | Amelia Earhart                         | Lockheed Vega 5B                        | No                        | Confirmed   | [10]    |
| h | Dick Smith                             | Bell JetRanger III *VH-DIK*             | No (and outside 1930s)    | Confirmed   | [11]    |

Notes:

- **a. P. G. Taylor + DH-6.** Sir Patrick Gordon ("Bill") Taylor's
  career is associated with the *Southern Cross* (as navigator with
  Kingsford Smith), the Lockheed Altair *Faith in Australia*, and
  flying-boats - not a DH-6. The Australian DH-6 register entries [4]
  do not list him. Treat as a red herring.
- **b. Smith brothers' Vimy.** The Cootamundra town timeline records
  "1920 - 23 February arrival of Ross and Keith Smith after the first
  flight from England to Australia." [3] Note this is the Australian
  victory tour after their 10 Dec 1919 arrival at Darwin, not the race
  itself.
- **c. Hinkler.** During his post-record tour of the southern states,
  "After stopping at Cootamundra for half an hour, Hinkler made his way
  to Wagga" (Saturday 17 March 1928), before continuing to Melbourne
  the next morning. [5]
- **d. KSM / Southern Cross VH-USU.** The aircraft only carried the
  Australian registration VH-USU from 5 April 1931 (it was G-AUSU
  4 July 1928 - 3 July 1929 and US-marked "1985" before that) [6][7].
  A Cootamundra landing under VH-USU markings is highly plausible given
  the aircraft's heavy 1931-1934 Australian touring, but I did not find
  a web source naming Cootamundra explicitly.
- **e. KLM Uiver.** The Uiver Memorial photo album captions an image
  "The KLM 'Uiver' DC-2 at Cootamundra on the return flight to the
  Netherlands" - this is the post-race return ferry late in 1934, not
  the race itself (the race emergency landing was at Albury). [8]
- **f. Freda Thompson.** Her 1934 solo UK->Australia flight was in a
  **DH-60 Moth Major** (G-ACUC / VH-UUC, *Christopher Robin*), not a
  DH-87 Hornet Moth. She acquired a Hornet Moth (*Christopher Robin II*)
  only after WWII, so a 1930s "Freda Thompson in a Hornet Moth" at
  Cootamundra is not possible. [9]
- **g. Earhart.** Her final flight reached Lae, New Guinea (June 1937);
  she disappeared on the next leg toward Howland Island and never
  reached the Australian mainland. [10]
- **h. Dick Smith.** His Bell 206B JetRanger III circumnavigation was
  1982-83, not the 1930s. The route into Sydney came in from Darwin
  via the north, and the departing Australian leg in May 1983 went
  Sydney -> Cape York -> Manila; no Cootamundra stop is recorded. [11]

## Item 12 - RAAF aircraft that operated from Cootamundra, 1940-1946

Cootamundra was home to **No. 1 Air Observers School (No. 1 AOS)** from
15 April 1940 until the unit re-formed at Evans Head, NSW, on
9 December 1943, and **No. 73 (Reserve) Squadron** was formed on the
base on 1 July 1942. **No. 60 Squadron** also operated from
Cootamundra during the war. [12][13][14]

Confirmed type at Cootamundra:

- **Avro Anson** - the primary navigator-training aircraft of No. 1 AOS.
  First Ansons arrived 25 June 1940; the RAAF memorial at Cootamundra
  Airfield is topped by a steel-crafted Anson, the type specifically
  used to train navigators there. [12][13]

Other types associated with No. 1 AOS (Fairey Battle, Lockheed Ventura,
Fairey Gannet, DH.82 Tiger Moth, DH.83 Fox Moth) appear in the unit
history **after** the move to Evans Head in December 1943 [12], so
they are out of scope for "aircraft to operate from Cootamundra". I
have **not** found a web-published full list of the aircraft flown by
No. 60 and No. 73 (Reserve) Squadrons while at Cootamundra - see
[Open questions](#open-questions-for-the-user).

| h | ~~Australia National Airways~~   | ~~No - ANA's Sydney-Melbourne ran via Canberra, not Cootamundra~~     | Confirmed     | [20]       |
| i | ~~REX Airlines~~                 | ~~No - Cootamundra's 1991-2002 carrier was Country Connection; Rex doesn't serve it~~ | Confirmed | [15][21] |

Notes:

- **a. Butler Air Transport.** "The airfield became the initial base
  for Butler Air Transport, established as an airmail contractor in
  1934 to operate ... between Cootamundra and Charleville ... The
  company relocated its base to Sydney in 1938 following the withdrawal
  of the airmail contract." [15] Arthur Butler's biography confirms he
  won the Charleville-Cootamundra section of the England-Australia
  airmail and operated it with DH.84 Dragons. [16] Cootamundra
  Airport's terminal is named **Arthur Butler Terminal** in his honour.
- **b. Larkin Aircraft Services.** Hugh Larkin's company traded as the
  Larkin Aircraft Supply Co. (LASCO); its airline subsidiary was
  Australian Aerial Services. The same operator therefore covers both
  options 13b and 13g. [17][18]
- **c. Masling.** "In November 1982, aviation company Masling
  Industries is formed" in Cootamundra; restructured June 1993 after
  the death of its owner. [3]
- **d. Airland.** No Australian airline by this name appears in the
  aviation-history sources searched. Treat as a red herring.
- **e. TAA.** Trans Australian Airlines (1946-1986) operated trunk
  routes (Sydney-Melbourne, Sydney-Brisbane, etc.) and the major
  regional hubs; none of the sources searched lists Cootamundra.
- **f. East-West Airlines.** A Tamworth-based regional founded 1947.
  Curtis Madsen's Sydney-Cootamundra-Temora-West Wyalong service
  ceased in mid-November 1950 and the aircraft were "shortly afterwards
  sold to East-West"; this is the closest published East-West /
  Cootamundra connection I found. [19] If you have an East-West
  timetable that lists Cootamundra as a scheduled stop it would upgrade
  this from Inferred to Confirmed.
- **g. Australian Aerial Services.** Larkin's AAS launched the
  Adelaide-Sydney airmail and passenger service on 2 June 1924, flying
  via Mildura, Hay, Narrandera and Cootamundra; from July 1925 the
  service was shortened to terminate at Cootamundra (Sydney connection
  by train). [17][18]
- **h. Australian National Airways.** The Holyman/ANA Sydney-Melbourne
  service from October 1935 operated via **Canberra**, not Cootamundra
  [20]. The earlier 1930 ANA (Kingsford Smith / Ulm) also flew trunk
  routes. No Cootamundra service appears in either airline's published
  history.
- **i. REX Airlines.** Regional Express was formed in August 2002 from
  the remains of Hazelton and Kendell after Ansett's collapse [21].
  Cootamundra's regional airline 1991-2002 was Country Connection
  Airlines [15], and Rex's published network does not include
  Cootamundra.
